Origins and location

I was born in Belgium, in the Charleroi area, in 1996. In 2010 (aged 14), I moved to Switzerland with part of my family. But change doesn’t scare me. To date, I’ve moved about twenty times. As soon as I was able, I went through the naturalisation process and have held a Swiss passport since 2020. Despite the respect I have for my roots, I feel much more at home in Switzerland, particularly in the Valais. I currently live in Renens whilst studying for my Master’s degree, Digital Experience Design at ECAL.

Secondary education and German

Moving to a new country had an impact on my education. In Belgium, the second language taught at school is Dutch. To catch up, I had to learn English and German at the same time. In order to be able to attend "college" school (gymnasium), I had to repeat the second year of "cycle" (first part of secondary school). During my time at secondary school, I spent a year in St Gallen at a private school, which was not counted towards my curriculum. I came away with a Goethe B1 certificate. This transitional year gave me the opportunity to change from "collège" of St-Maurice to Sion.

At secondary school, I chose the Visual Arts option. Given the high standard of Swiss education, I was worried I wouldn’t be able to keep up with the maths. I’d always got very good marks in maths, but I’ve always loved being creative too (drawing, crafts, etc.). In 2017, I took a career aptitude test with a psychologist. Health comes first

Straight after I finished secondary school (2017), I decided to apply to EPFL to study computer science, even though I hadn’t taken any math option at school. I took some summer courses, but that wasn’t enough. It started to affect my health (migraines) and that wasn't right. So I dropped out after the first semester (under the system at the time, I would have been permanently expelled if I had failed in the second semester). Wanting to look after my health and as I had the time, I've really thrown myself into fitness. I took the Fitness Instructor course with Fitspro, to learn the basics so I wouldn’t get injured and could keep learning throughout this second semester.

Give up IT?

I asked myself a lot of questions early 2018. I enrolled in biomedical studies and the HES health programme for next year, convinced that IT wasn’t for me. My sister, knowing me, suggested I do an internship in the IT department at the City of Sion, where she works. These brilliant colleagues, business IT specialists, welcomed me on a short internship and I loved it. I then cancelled my enrollments and enrolled part-time at the Sierre University of Applied Sciences (HES) in Business IT. I applied to work at the City of Sion in their department so that I could work alongside my studies.

Business IT and English

I must admit I also chose the part-time option because the course was taught in French, unlike the full-time programme (at the time). My level of English and German was really poor when I left secondary school. But English was gradually introduced into the course. I then worked for two years as a research assistant, where I had to use English in European project meetings. I also taught courses in English. The ECAL DXD Master’s programme is also taught in English with an international class. So the practice never stops.

My priorities

I’ve always been passionate about my work, whether it was helping others when I was doing technical support for the City of Sion, or creating things through coding or design . But my health will always come first. I like working on things where I understand the purpose: why are we doing this? For whom? On what scale? This gives me a lot of motivation, and the work involved often leads to improvements that help achieve the objectives. This need for purpose is what led me to UX. To deliver the best possible product, you need to understand the users, their objectives and their motivations.

Working style

I’m a firm believer in agility. My working group at the HES used SCRUM on Tuleap, but the team struggled to get to work with it. So I pushed for and implemented a migration to Azure DevOps. I really liked that way of working. That said, I don’t think you need to use all the SCRUM tools. this must be tailored to each project. I enjoy working in a team and having my ideas challenged. Making decisions as a team (of technical people) allows us, in my view, to make the best decisions for long-term durability.

And outside of work?

As a child, I loved reading, brain teasers and drawing, and today I love puzzle or narrative video games. I love creating things; I even went through a cosplay phase. As I mentioned earlier, I like to keep up to date with healthcare, particularly mental health. But my passion these days is motorcycling. It’s the first time in my life that I’ve found an activity that allows my thoughts to focus. I love having a mind full of thoughts because it allows me to be creative, but it’s good to switch off sometimes. I also do track riding, even though it terrifies me. I’m stepping out of my comfort zone and improving my decision-making skills.
